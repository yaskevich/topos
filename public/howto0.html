How to use. Please read!

To search via Russian spelling, enter into search field either full place name, or its segment. Keep in mind, that Russian orthography does not reflect akanye, that's why it's more related to etymology. So, Абра́мава and Абрамо́ва do differ in Belarusian, but they look the same in Russian.

To search via Belarusian spelling, add a @ character (without space).

Каб пошукавы запыт адпавядаў пачатку назвы, дадайце клічнік ! перад запытам, каб адпавядаў канцу назвы, дадайце клічнік ! пасля запыта.

Keep in mind that the data for that visualization are mostly taken from open sources that are compiled automatcially and/or fixed/completed by volunteers. The state does not publish relevant information in machine-readable form. That's why the dataset can contain orthographic mistakes, translation issues; some existing place can be absent, be duplicated or not georeferenced.

If you conduct place name research or read a discipline related to geography or toponymy, take into account that this tool does not guarantee data accuracy, therefore the fidelity of the results based on the automatically generetaed visualization.

Besides, obviously, search via mere coincidence that is implemented here does not consider morphology or etymology of place names! E.g., searching for suffix of the so called „Safarewicz border“ (the territory inhabited in ancient times by Baltic tribes) by means of the query -shki outputs both Антонішкі (OK) and Шышкі (error!). It is more complicated with semantics: e.g., searching for radices related to 'red' (Standard Russian ‑красн‑ vs Standard Belarusian ‑чырвон‑) does not guarantee that all they are results of Soviet politics. Many units are older than Soviet or even ancient Belarusian names.

If you are interested in extending and improving Belarusian geospatial datatset, join the discussion on standardization and linguistic research (alyaxey.yaskevich@gmail.com) or directly gathering and verifying of the data in terms of the OpenStreetMap project.
